Norwegian Occupation - WW2

Introduction

The German occupation of Norway during World War 2 is a significant event in the country's history. From 1940 to 1945, Norway was under German military control, which had a profound impact on the country and its people. During this period, Norwegians were subjected to harsh living conditions, censorship, and forced labor. Despite this, the resistance movement in Norway grew and eventually contributed to the liberation of the country. The story of Norway during the war is one of bravery, sacrifice, and endurance in the face of adversity. This website aims to provide a detailed account of the German occupation of Norway, including the events leading up to it, the impact on the country and its people, and the eventual liberation.

1940

April 9th; Germany Invades

The initial German invasion of Norway during World War II, also known as Operation Weserübung, began on April 9, 1940. It was a surprise attack that targeted several strategic locations in Norway simultaneously. German forces successfully captured key ports, airfields, and communication centers in southern and central Norway. The Norwegian military and allied forces fought back fiercely, but were eventually overwhelmed by the superior firepower and organization of the German army. The invasion was a major success for the Germans, as it secured access to Norwegian resources such as iron ore and enabled them to establish bases for the U-boat war in the North Atlantic. It also marked the beginning of a long and difficult occupation of Norway by the German forces.

Battle of Narvik

The Battle of Narvik was a significant naval battle that occurred during the early stages of World War II. In April 1940, German forces launched a surprise attack on Norway, and a large German naval task force was sent to capture the strategic port of Narvik. However, a British naval force arrived shortly thereafter, and a fierce battle ensued. Despite being outnumbered, the British managed to gain the upper hand, sinking several German ships and forcing the rest to withdraw. The battle marked the first major Allied victory of the war and helped to prevent the Germans from gaining control of Norway's iron ore, which was critical to their war effort.

Sinking of the Blücher

The sinking of the German cruiser Blücher is a significant event that occurred during the early stages of the German invasion of Norway in 1940. On April 9, 1940, German forces attempted to capture the Norwegian capital of Oslo by sea, using the Blücher to transport troops and supplies. However, the Norwegian coastal fortress of Oscarsborg fired upon the Blücher, causing damage to the vessel and forcing it to ground. Despite efforts to save the ship, the Blücher eventually sank, with many German soldiers and crew members lost. The sinking of the Blücher was a major blow to the German invasion effort and helped delay their capture of Oslo. It remains a notable event in the history of the Norwegian resistance during World War II.

1941

The Atlantik Wall

The Atlantikwall was a massive coastal defense system constructed by the Germans during World War II to protect their occupied territories from Allied invasion. Norway, which was occupied by the Germans, was an important location for the construction of the Atlantikwall. The construction of the Norwegian portion of the Atlantikwall began in the fall of 1941 and continued until the end of the war. The wall consisted of a network of bunkers, gun emplacements, minefields, and other fortifications. The construction of the Atlantikwall was a massive undertaking, and required the labor of thousands of forced laborers from occupied countries, as well as local Norwegian workers. Despite the extensive fortifications, the Atlantikwall was ultimately breached by the Allies, leading to the liberation of Norway and other parts of Europe from German occupation.

Early Resistance

In 1941, the Norwegian Resistance movement was in its early stages. After the German occupation of Norway in 1940, many Norwegians were left feeling disillusioned and helpless. However, a small group of individuals refused to accept the German occupation and began organizing a resistance movement. These individuals came from all walks of life and included both men and women. They carried out acts of sabotage, gathered intelligence, and worked to disrupt German operations in Norway. Despite facing immense risks, they continued their efforts to resist the occupation and fight for their country's freedom. As the war continued, the Norwegian Resistance would grow in strength and become an important force in the fight against the Germans.

Sinking of the Bismark Battleship

Norway played a significant role in the sinking of the German battleship Bismarck during World War II. The Bismarck was one of the largest and most powerful battleships in the German navy, and its sinking was a major victory for the Allies. In May 1941, the Bismarck and the heavy cruiser Prinz Eugen were making their way to the North Atlantic to attack Allied shipping when they were spotted by British aircraft. The British Royal Navy quickly dispatched several warships, including the battleship HMS King George V and the battlecruiser HMS Hood, to intercept the German vessels. In a fierce battle on May 24th, the Hood was hit and sunk by the Bismarck's guns, killing all but three of its crew. However, the Bismarck was damaged by the King George V and later torpedoed and sunk by British aircraft, with Norwegian ships also taking part in the pursuit. The sinking of the Bismarck was a significant victory for the Allies and marked a turning point in the naval war in the Atlantic.

1942

Heavy Water Sabotage

The Heavy Water Sabotage at Vemork was a significant event during World War II that took place on February 27, 1942. The operation was carried out by a team of Norwegian commandos, who managed to infiltrate the Vemork heavy water plant, which was controlled by the Germans. The plant produced heavy water, which is used in nuclear reactions, and was a key component in the German nuclear weapons program. The commandos were able to successfully destroy the heavy water production facility, effectively slowing down the German nuclear program. This sabotage mission was a pivotal moment in the Norwegian Resistance movement, and demonstrated the bravery and commitment of the Norwegian people in their fight against Nazi occupation.

Occupation of Svalbard

During World War II, Germany occupied the Norwegian archipelago of Svalbard, which is located in the Arctic Ocean. The occupation began on September 8, 1941, when a German naval force arrived in the archipelago and captured the small Norwegian garrison stationed there. The Germans quickly established a meteorological station and a radio station, both of which were crucial for their military operations in the region. They also made efforts to mine the coal on the islands, which was an important resource for their war effort. Despite the German presence, the local population of Svalbard managed to maintain a degree of resistance, and a number of Norwegians were able to escape to the mainland or to neighboring countries to join the Allied forces. The German occupation of Svalbard ended on September 4, 1945, following the German surrender at the end of World War II.

Operation Fritham

Operation Fritham was a joint British-Norwegian operation during World War II that aimed to establish a resistance network in occupied Norway. It was launched in June 1941, and the main objective was to establish radio contact between Norway and London to enable effective communication and coordination of resistance activities. The operation involved dropping trained Norwegian agents and equipment by parachute from British aircraft into remote areas of Norway, primarily in the north. While the operation initially faced some setbacks due to poor weather conditions and the difficulties of operating in such a harsh environment, it eventually succeeded in establishing a network of resistance fighters who were able to carry out sabotage and intelligence-gathering operations against the occupying German forces. The success of Operation Fritham played a significant role in the eventual liberation of Norway in 1945.

1943

The Milorg Resistance

In 1943, the Norwegian resistance movement against the German occupation grew in strength and organization with the formation of the Milorg (Military Organization) resistance group. The group was founded by Colonel Jens Christian Hauge, who had returned to Norway from exile in London with the aim of strengthening and unifying the scattered resistance groups that had formed across the country. Under his leadership, Milorg developed a hierarchical structure and established communication networks, training programs, and supply chains. The group also carried out sabotage missions, including attacks on German troop transports and facilities involved in the war effort. The formation of Milorg marked a turning point in the Norwegian resistance movement, providing a more cohesive and effective challenge to the German occupation.

Norwegian Jewish Deportation

In 1943, the German occupying forces in Norway began deporting Norwegian Jews to concentration camps. The first group of 532 Jews were arrested on October 26, 1942, and were sent to the Auschwitz concentration camp in occupied Poland. Over the course of the next year, more than 700 Norwegian Jews were deported. This was done as part of the Nazi's "Final Solution," which aimed to eliminate the Jewish population of Europe. The deportation was carried out with the help of the Norwegian police, who assisted the Germans in identifying and arresting Jews. The deportation was a tragic event in Norwegian history, and it is estimated that only a handful of Norwegian Jews survived the war.

Sinking of the SS Palatia

The SS Palatia was a German cargo ship that was sunk during World War II. On December 14, 1943, the Palatia was on a voyage from Norway to Germany when it was attacked by a British submarine. The submarine fired torpedoes at the Palatia, hitting it and causing it to sink. The crew of the Palatia attempted to abandon ship, but many were killed in the attack. Of the 115 crew members on board, only 11 survived the sinking. The sinking of the Palatia was just one of many instances of German ships being attacked by British submarines during the war, as the Allies attempted to disrupt German supply lines and hinder their war effort.

1944

The Liberation of Falstad

The Falstad concentration camp was located in the municipality of Levanger, in the Trøndelag county of Norway. The camp was established by the Germans in November 1941 and primarily housed political prisoners and resistance fighters. Conditions in the camp were harsh and many prisoners suffered from malnutrition and disease. In May 1945, as the German occupation of Norway was coming to an end, the camp was liberated by the advancing Soviet Red Army. The remaining prisoners were freed and the Norwegian authorities took control of the camp. After the war, the camp was used to house German prisoners of war and collaborators. Today, the site is a memorial and museum, dedicated to the memory of those who suffered and died at Falstad.

Liberation of Finnmark

The 1944 Liberation of Finnmark was a significant event during the German occupation of Norway. Finnmark, a county in northern Norway, was occupied by German forces in 1940. During the war, Finnmark became a battleground between German and Soviet forces, resulting in significant destruction and displacement of the local population. In October 1944, Soviet forces launched an offensive to liberate the region, with support from Norwegian forces and the British Royal Navy. The Germans put up fierce resistance, but by November, they had retreated from Finnmark, leaving behind a devastated region. The liberation of Finnmark marked the end of the war in Norway and the beginning of the rebuilding process for the region.

The Lofoten Raid

The 1944 Lofoten Raid was a military operation carried out by the British against the German occupation forces in Norway during World War II. On March 4th, 1943, a force of over 500 British soldiers landed on the Lofoten Islands off the northern coast of Norway. Their mission was to destroy German fish oil factories and other military targets, gather intelligence, and capture German troops. The raid was a success, with the British destroying several factories, capturing over 200 German soldiers, and killing several others. The operation also helped to boost morale in Norway and demonstrated to the Germans that their occupation was not invincible. The Lofoten Raid was one of several commando raids carried out by the Allies against the German occupation forces in Norway during the war.
